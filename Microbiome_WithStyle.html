Phylum-level Analysis Can Distinguish Skin Microbial Communities

Human and environmental microbiome studies use high-throughput DNA sequencing to characterize microbial communities present on and in the human body. The sequences obtained are classified by comparing them to a database of known sequences. Hierarchical biological groupings are known as taxonomy. Taxonomic ranks range from very large categories, such as Phylum and Order, which contain many organisms with varying degrees of relatedness, to smaller, fine-grained categories, such as Genus and Species, which contain organisms that are very closely related [1].

Fierer et al. compared microbial communities on the fingertips and computer keyboards of 3 individuals, demonstrating that differences in microbiomes between individuals were far greater than differences between individuals [2]. Microbial classification was performed by first clustering sequences to form operational taxonomic units (OTUs), which are a specific taxonomic indicator, approximately equivalent to the species level [2]. OTUs were then compared to a sequence database to assign taxonomy [2]. Comparisons between communities at different skin and keyboard sites were calculated using Principal Components Analysis (PCA) based on Unifrac distances [2]. Unifrac is a metric that accounts for phylogenetic relationships between organisms â€“ this means that two communities may not contain the same organisms, but if they contain similar organisms, they are considered to be more closely related [3].

Purpose of the current work

Unifrac distances can be difficult both to compute and understand, and OTU level classifications can provide hundreds to thousands of variables as input to PCA. The purpose of the current exploration was to determine if the unique signals in individual skin microbiomes were still apparent using coarse-grained Phylum-level classifications as input to PCA with standard Euclidean distances. The data was filtered to include only the 8 most abundant phyla, and raw sequence counts were normalized to relative abundances to account for varying sequencing efforts between samples.

Summary and Conclusions

Microbial communities were characteristic of individuals as opposed to their site of origin, and these differences were driven by discernable variations in relative abundances at coarse-grained taxonomic levels. OTU determination and classification can be fraught with inconsistencies related to clustering algorithms as well as the content of databases used to assign taxonomy, which may not provide taxonomic resolution at the same level for all sequences and do not always agree on classifications [5], [6]. Our work demonstrates that Phylum-level classifications, which are larger in scope and are more likely to agree across methods, preserved the microbial community differences observed in the original study. This suggests that more generally, microbiome study data can be aggregated from initial OTU-level analysis to higher order taxonomic groupings without dramatic loss of drivers of community difference.
